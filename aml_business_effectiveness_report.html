Tuning and Model Objective

Rule tuning was applied to R1 Amount on the validation split. Candidate percentiles were 95.0%, 97.5%, 99.0%, and 99.5%. The objective was to reduce alert rate subject to an 80% recall guardrail. None of the candidates met the recall floor, so the 95.0% threshold was retained because it gave the highest recall.

The model objective was alert-level prioritisation: rank already-alerted transactions by likelihood of is_laundering, optimising Precision@1,000 on validation. LightGBM and XGBoost were tuned with Optuna for 50 trials each; class imbalance was handled by preserving all positives and sampling negatives at a 10:1 ratio.

The selected extended model was LightGBM, with 53 selected features out of 229 candidates. The model is intended to prioritise review, not auto-close or suppress alerts.

Conclusion

The AML detection framework is directionally strong for prioritisation: the extended LightGBM model improves the ability to concentrate known laundering cases in the top review queues, and the final P1 queue is operationally sized with high precision.

The key weakness is operational volume. The deterministic rule layer alerts on nearly half of all transactions, and several rules contribute large volumes at very low precision. This prevents full promotion readiness despite successful model, explainability, case consolidation, and stress-testing gates.

Recommended decision: keep the extended model as the preferred prioritisation challenger and continue remediation before production promotion. The next management action should be rule-volume remediation, especially R4, R6, and R1, plus formal handling of P1 overflow and ongoing temporal stability monitoring.
